\section*{Abstract}
This paper studies how information structure and emotions affect co-operation in infinitely repeated public goods games with communication. We conduct a laboratory experiment in which groups of four participants play a series of probabilistically-ending super games. The key treatment variations are in feedback: in one treatment participants observe both individual contributions and the group total, while in the other they only observe the group total. Participants exchange chat message before making their contribution decisions. We record facial expressions to measure emotional responses using facial emotion recognition and text sentiment analysis. Preliminary results show higher average contributions when only aggregate contributions are observable. Communication helps identify behavioral patterns such as promise-making, lying, and “sucker” behavior. While promises themselves do not significantly predict contributions due to ceiling effects, being exploited by a lying group member significantly reduces subsequent contributions. Sentiment analysis further shows that more positive communication is associated with higher contributions, highlighting the role of emotions and communication in sustaining cooperation under different monitoring structures.

\section{Introduction}
Cooperation in public goods games critically depends on both communication as well as the information individuals have about one another’s behavior. The introduction of communication and feedback structures in public good games comes from the understanding that social preferences may affect strategic behavior. If people are affected by information as well as by others' behavior, allowing feedback could enable reciprocity, informal punishment, and sustain cooperation and accountability over a long period of time. A large body of experimental literature has shown that allowing subjects to communicate can substantially increase contributions relative to settings without communication, even when communication is cheap talk and non-binding. More importantly, the degree of transparency surrounding individual actions - such as whether individual contributions are observable or only aggregate outcomes are revealed - has been shown to shape incentives, social norms, and the sustainability of cooperation. Despite the extensive study of these two mechanisms independently, less is known about how communication interacts with informational visibility to influence cooperative behavior over time.

In many real-world collective action problems, individuals can communicate freely while facing varying degrees of transparency about their own and others' behavior. In some settings, such as small teams or committees, individual contributions are highly visible. In others, such as large organizations or anonymous online platforms, only aggregate outcomes are observed, obscuring individual responsibility. Understanding how cooperation emerges and evolves under these different informational regimes, particularly when communication is present,is essential for explaining variation in cooperative outcomes across different institutional environments.

In this paper, we present an experimental study to explain how the visibility of individual contributions affects cooperation, communication, and contribution dynamics in a repeated public goods game with endogenous communication. We employ a between-subjects design with two treatments that vary in terms of informational transparency. In the \textbf{Individual Feedback (IF)} treatment, subjects observe the individual contributions of each group member at the end of each period. In the \textbf{Aggregate Feedback (AF)} treatment, subjects observe only the total, aggregate group contribution. In both treatments, subjects communicate freely via an on-screen chat before making each decision. This design allows us to isolate how transparency at the individual versus group level interacts with communication to shape cooperative behavior.

Our experimental environment incorporates several features that reflect realistic collective decision-making settings. Subjects interact repeatedly within supergames but are re-matched across supergames, limiting the scope for long-term reputation building, while preserving short-run strategic considerations. Communication is unrestricted and occurs prior to each contribution decision, allowing groups to coordinate, make promises, and express approval or disapproval. Finally, the random-ending structure within each supergame mitigates endgame effects and encourages forward-looking behavior throughout the interaction.

The contribution of this paper is threefold. First, we provide causal evidence on how informational visibility alters contribution levels and dynamics when communication is available. To reiterate, previous studies have examined the effects of communication or visibility in isolation. Our design directly compares individual-level and aggregate-level transparency within an otherwise identical communicative environment. Second, we analyze how communication content and patterns differ across informational regimes, shedding light on the mechanisms through which transparency influences cooperation. Third, we examine how cooperation evolves over time within and across supergames, highlighting differences in stability, responsiveness to deviations, and decay in contributions.

The emotional state of a player can be a significant driver of how they make certain decisions. Within the public good framework, with communication allowed, we are interested in learning two things: Does communication, and subsequent match or mismatch of action trigger emotional response in participants. If emotions are present, do they get reflected in behavior? To address these questions, we record our participants through the duration of our study and analyze these recorded videos via a facial recognition technology. This allows us to see if there is any correlation between verbal communication and facial expressions, and between emotions and contributions in the public goods game. Our use of facial recognition is motivated by the idea that emotions are often revealed through subtle, involuntary facial expressions that may not be explicitly stated in verbal communication. Unlike self-reported measures of emotions, which may suffer from recall bias, the technology allows us to map real time expressions to strategic interactions. We record facial expression over chat duration and while subjects make their contribution decisions to monitor their reactions to chat, as well as self and others' contributions. We link the facial expression data with both behavioral data and chat data. We first analyze whether specific communication patterns such as promising to cooperate or lying are associated with distinct emotional expressions. Second, we examine whether measured emotions are reflect in subsequent contribution behavior.

The remainder of the paper proceeds as follows. Section 2 reviews the related literature. Section 3 describes the experimental design and procedures. Section 4 presents the main results on contributions and communication. Section 5 discusses robustness and additional analyses. Section 6 concludes.

\section{Related Literature}

\subsection{Information}

%why do we not put any cost for participation?

Public good games provide a standard framework to understand cooperation as well as free-riding behavior in group settings. 
In this framework, individuals are endowed with some resource and asked to contribute to a common pool. Contributions are multiplied by a factor greater than one and redistributed equally across all group members. The private return from contributing to the pool is socially beneficial but privately costly if Marginal Per Capita Return (MPCR) is set below 1. While communication was initially believed to increase social contributions even though it comes at a private cost, early experimental work on public good games with feedback structures shows mixed results on how individual or aggregate feedback promotes cooperation.
\cite{sell1991levels} is the first paper to experimentally investigate the effects of individual versus aggregate feedback in public goods game. It is the only paper that finds larger contributions when subjects were provided with individual feedback. This study used groups of four subjects with $MPCR = 0.75$, and ran the study with a relatively small sample size (32 subjects in the AF and 31 in IF treatment) with fixed partners for 10 continuous periods. The key contribution from this paper was showing that when actions are observable at the individual level, overall contributions are higher. A second study by \cite{weimann1994individual} used an even smaller sample with 15 subjects in each treatment with groups of five subjects and an $MPCR = 0.5$, and the same partners were matched for 10 periods. In the treatment with feedback (TF), average contributions were reported rather than total, which could be an interesting component in affecting behavior, but the sample size is too small to have a statistical power. Similarly, \cite{croson2001feedback} also use fixed partners over 10 periods with 24 subjects in each treatment with groups of 4 subjects. They also used an $MPCR = 0.5$. These early studies set the groundwork for incorporating feedback in pubic good games, but the use of relatively small sample size and short time horizon motivates the need for further analysis.

Over the years, the literature on public good games with feedback has grown to include larger sample sizes and improved designs. However, most studies find no statistically significant differences between IF and AF treatments, but they note an important difference in the distribution of behavior, particularly in terms of variance and cooperative types:

\cite{croson2001feedback} finds no statistical difference in group contributions across feedback treatments, but the study reports that the variance of between-group contributions is significantly higher under individual feedback. Additionally, the study reported more free riders in the IF treatment, as well as more cooperation. This suggests that individual feedback perhaps amplifies heterogeneity in behavior; some respond positively to observed individual behavior, while others exploit the cooperative group members.\cite{carpenter2004rome} extend this analysis using a larger sample (80 and 85 subjects per treatment). They match subjects in group of five separately each period for 10 periods, and use an $MPCR = 0.75$. The key difference in their design is that matching happens between strangers, which reduces repeated-game incentives. They classify free riders based on three thresholds: contributions less than one-fourth, one-third or one-half of endowment, and report there were either all free rides under the IF treatment or a situation where all cooperated. Although this paper does not directly report contribution levels, it helps identify behavioral types. 

These studies suggest that feedback structure primarily affects the distribution and coordination of behaviors, with aggregate feedback showing mixed strategies within groups and individual feedback increasing divergence in behavior. More recent experiments have added to the comparisons between individual and aggregate feedback by analyzing features such as punishment, imitation, and matching procedures.\\
\cite{bigoni2012feedback} study how imitation and learning interact with feedback structures. In their paper, they show that when individuals can observe other's actions directly, they may imitate successful strategies, leading to strategic adjustment based on how much information is available.
\cite{nikiforakis2010feedback} show that targeted punishment can sustain cooperation under individual feedback, but because effectiveness of punishment depends on the ability to identify individual behavior, punishment is not useful under aggregate feedback.\cite{cox2015framing} show in their paper that feedback not only affects behavior but also beliefs about others' contributions, meaning belief formation as well as expectations should be considered while framing feedback structures. \cite{cason1999laboratory} compare environments with no feedback, while \cite{irlenbusch2019designing} explore partial information structures, where only one contribution (the maximum, the minimum, or random) per group is revealed. These works show that minimal informational/ any transparency influences behavior.

\subsection{Communication}

\cite{Isaac1988_Communication} first introduced communication in a repeated public good game and showed that even limited opportunities to discuss before making contribution decisions can affect the contribution amounts significantly. Other early experimental studies such as \cite{Dawes1977_Communication} also demonstrate that allowing participants to communicate leads to higher contributions and improved group outcomes relative to a no-communication baselines. \cite{Sally1995_Meta} provides a comprehensive meta-analysis of experiments conducted over several decades, showing that communication consistently increases cooperation and reduces free-riding behavior. \cite{wilson1997liar} further examine the strategic nature of communication, showing that communication can be effective even if it is not truthful. Their study shows that even when messages are potentially deceptive, communication can improve coordination, implying that communication helps to increase information exchange and also creates ability to influence expectation of other's behavior.
\cite{cason1999laboratory} used verbal communication analysis and find that allowing participants to communicate directly through verbal or face to face interactions increases cooperation relative to no communication or restrictive communication environments. These findings challenged the prediction of standard self-interest models and highlighted the importance of social interaction and norm formation in collective action problems. Overall, communication allows participants to coordinate expectations, articulate shared goals, and invoke social norms, thereby mitigating strategic uncertainty and fostering trust. Moreover, communication can stabilize/sustain cooperation rather than decay of contribution over periods as in a standard public good scenario.

More recent research has shifted focus toward understanding the mechanisms through which communication operates. \cite{Brandts2019_Communication} survey the experimental literature on communication and emphasize that its effectiveness depends critically on institutional details, including the information available to participants and the structure of interaction. Communication can facilitate cooperation by enabling coordination on strategies, reinforcing social norms, and expressing approval or disapproval, but its impact is not uniform across environments.

The timing and structure of communication also matter. \cite{Oprea2014_ContinuousTime} study continuous-time communication in a public goods experiment and find that richer communication opportunities improve coordination and reduce early misalignment of beliefs. Their results suggest that communication serves as an active coordination device, allowing participants to update expectations rather than relying on static messages.

When individual contributions are observable, participants can condition their behavior on others’ actions, enabling informal reciprocity, reputation building, and norm enforcement. \cite{Fischbacher2001_Conditional} provide seminal evidence that many individuals are conditionally cooperative, increasing their contributions when others contribute more and reducing them when others free ride. Such behavior relies fundamentally on the ability to observe or infer others’ actions.

Visibility also interacts with social preferences and framing effects. \cite{Andreoni1995_Cooperation} shows that cooperation can be influenced by how decisions are framed, highlighting the role of moral considerations and social image. When individual behavior is observable, social image concerns and accountability are likely to be more salient, strengthening incentives to cooperate.

More broadly, the ability to monitor and respond to individual behavior has been shown to support the emergence of informal governance structures. \cite{Ostrom1992_Covenants} demonstrate that groups can sustain cooperation through self-governance mechanisms even in the absence of formal enforcement, provided that individual actions are sufficiently transparent. Similarly, \cite{Gachter2007_Norms} show that mechanisms enabling norm enforcement generate long-run benefits for cooperation, underscoring the importance of accountability in sustaining collective action. In contrast, when only aggregate outcomes are observable, individual responsibility is blurred. This may weaken conditional cooperation and reduce the effectiveness of informal sanctions, leading to more rapid decay in contributions.

While both communication and informational visibility have been shown to promote cooperation, fewer studies examine how these mechanisms interact. Communication may substitute for visibility by allowing individuals to signal intentions and coordinate behavior, or it may depend on visibility to be effective by enabling verification and accountability.

\cite{Haruvy2017_CommunicationVisibility} directly investigate this interaction by varying the visibility of individual contributions in a public goods game with communication. They find that communication is substantially more effective when individual contributions are observable, suggesting that transparency and communication are complementary. When individual actions are hidden, communication alone is less successful in sustaining cooperation.

\cite{Brandts2019_Communication} similarly emphasize that the impact of communication depends on the surrounding informational environment, noting that communication without the ability to attribute behavior may be insufficient to support stable cooperation. However, existing studies provide limited insight into how communication content and contribution dynamics differ across informational regimes, particularly in settings with repeated interaction and rematching.

Random-ending designs are commonly used to mitigate endgame effects and encourage forward-looking behavior. \cite{Isaac1988_Communication}study the dynamics of public goods provision using random continuation probabilities and show that such structures influence contribution trajectories and strategic adjustment over time. 

In this paper we combine repeated interaction within supergames, random rematching across supergames, and endogenous communication. This design allows us to study how cooperation evolves dynamically under different informational regimes, while limiting long-term reputation effects. While prior work establishes that both communication and visibility matter for cooperation, we show that the effectiveness of communication critically depends on the ability to attribute behavior to individuals. Moreover, we document how informational transparency shapes not only contribution levels but also the nature and function of communication itself.

\subsection{Emotion}

Emotions play a central role in shaping economic behavior, particularly in environments involving social interaction, cooperation, and strategic uncertainty (\cite{Loewenstein2000};\cite{Elster1998}). Standard models of self-interested behavior predict free riding in public goods games; however, experimental evidence consistently shows substantial deviations from this prediction. One explanation for these deviations is that individuals experience emotional responses—such as guilt, anger, or satisfaction—that influence their decisions \cite{Battigalli2007}. In public goods settings, contributions often reflect not only material payoffs but also emotional reactions to others’ behavior. For instance, individuals may experience positive emotions when contributing to a cooperative group or negative emotions when observing free riding. These emotional responses can reinforce or undermine cooperative behavior over time \cite{Fehr2002}

A large body of literature documents that individuals are conditionally cooperative, meaning they adjust their contributions based on others’ actions \cite{Fischbacher2001}. While this behavior is often modeled in terms of preferences or beliefs, emotions provide an important underlying mechanism.

When individuals observe others contributing, they may experience positive affect such as trust or satisfaction, which reinforces cooperation. Conversely, observing free riding may trigger negative emotions such as anger or disappointment, leading to reduced contributions or retaliatory behavior \cite{Pillutla1996}. Thus, emotions can amplify the dynamics of conditional cooperation by strengthening responses to others’ actions.

Importantly, these emotional reactions depend on expectations. Violations of expectations, such as when a partner fails to follow through on a communicated commitment, are particularly likely to generate strong emotional responses \cite{Charness2006}. This suggests that communication and expectations formation play a critical role in shaping emotional dynamics.

Communication introduces an additional layer through which emotions can influence behavior. By allowing participants to make promises, express intentions, or invoke norms, communication creates expectations about future actions. When these expectations are met, participants may experience positive emotions that reinforce cooperation. When they are violated, participants may experience negative emotions that lead to punishment, withdrawal, or reduced contributions \cite{Fehr2002}.

Traditionally, experimental studies measure emotions using self-reported surveys or questionnaires administered after the decision-making period. While informative, these measures have limitations: they are often coarse, retrospective, and subject to reporting biases \cite{Mauss2009}.

More recent approaches incorporate physiological or behavioral measures, such as facial expressions, to capture emotional responses in real time. Facial expression analysis provides a non-invasive and continuous measure of affect, allowing researchers to observe how emotions evolve during interaction, rather than relying solely reports after the fact. (\cite{Ekman1992};\cite{Lewinski2014}).

By using facial recognition technology, researchers can identify patterns of emotional expression associated with specific events, such as communication exchanges or observed deviations from expected behavior. This approach enables a more precise mapping between emotional states and strategic decisions, while also complementing traditional behavioral and self-reported measures \cite{Barrett2019}.

\section{Experimental Design}
Our study employs a between-subjects design with two treatments that vary the degree of informational visibility in a standard public goods environment. Across the two treatments, we manipulate whether subjects can observe both aggregate and individual contributions of their group members or only the aggregate contribution of the group. In the \textbf{Individual Feedback (IF)} treatment, each subject observes the individual contribution of each group member at the end of every round, while in the \textbf{Aggregate Feedback (AF)} treatment, subjects see only the total contribution placed into the group account, without any breakdown of who contributed how much. This manipulation allows us to study how transparency, either at the individual or group level, affects cooperative behavior, communication patterns, and contribution dynamics.

Participants were paired into groups of four subjects. They were randomly assigned to one of the two treatments and remained in the same treatment for the entire session. Each participant was assigned a unique letter at the beginning of the session. In the IF treatment, these letters were used to allow participants to identify individuals and observe their behavior in the group. 
The experiment consisted of five super games, each made up of a varying number of rounds. Groups were re-matched across super games in a way that no subject remained with the same set of partners across super games. Each super game followed a random-ending structure based on the continuation probabilities used in \cite{lugovskyy2013repeatedpg} (Sequence 1). The sequence of period lengths was (3, 4, 3, 7, and 5 rounds), totaling 22 rounds over the duration of each experimental session. Each participant played all 22 rounds, resulting in 110 total rounds across the five super games. 

At the beginning of every round, each participant received 25 ECU (Experimental Currency Units) in their private account. Participants decided how many of these points to allocate to the group account. After each round, subjects viewed their own earnings from that round alongside the group account total (and individual contributions in the IF treatment). Our unique addition to the design included allowing participants to communicate as they moved through the session. Each participant was allowed to chat with the other group members before deciding their contribution to the group or to the private account. The chat page restarted in each round, and the total time allocated for chatting decreased over the course of a super game \footnote{Initial chat time was set to one minute.}. Subjects could freely exchange messages, but were prohibited from sharing identifying information or using profanity. All communication occurred through an on-screen chat box \footnote{Refer to Appendix: Chat Page to see a sample of how the chat box appeared on the screen for participants}.

After completing all five super games, one super game was randomly selected to determine each participant’s final payoff. We conducted a total of 10 sessions, each with 16 participants, yielding 160 subjects overall. Subjects were paid a participation fee of \$7.5 plus their earnings. \subsection{Communication}
During the experiment, we allowed participants to freely communicate in writing. We performed  text analysis over the content of each exchanged chat. Historically, classifying text has been arduous. However, recent advancements in large language models allows us to perform this task efficiently.
We analyze chats according to two dimensions: chat behavior and chat sentiment. 
\subsubsection{Chat Behavior}
To understand the chat behavior, we classify text based on promise-making and lying behavior and assess its effect on overall contributions. Specifically, we are interested in identifying whether a participant makes a promise to the  group members about contributing to the group account. Using \cite{openai_gpt5mini_2025}'s GPT-5-mini model, we classify a participant's message as a promise or not a promise.\footnote{ We use the same prompt for all chats with the following elements: a brief explanation of the public goods game, a list of examples of promises, an instruction to classify, and the 
preceding messages in the conversation. The model is explicitly told to take the participant's message in the context of the whole conversation.}

We then use the messages' classification to correspondingly classify subjects' behavior. We create two separate classifications at the participant-round level. First, we classify a participant as a ``liar'' if they made a promise to contribute to the group account, but failed to contribute more than 20 ECUs in the next contribution decision. Each participant begins a super game without the ``liar'' label. After a first lie, the  ``liar'' classification persists for the rest of the super game. We also classify a participant as a ``sucker'' if they contributed the maximum (25 ECUs) in a round where at least one group member lied. Similarly to the liar label, the sucker label persists for the rest of the super game once they first become a sucker.   &+ \alpha_s + \alpha_r \Big).
\end{align}


The second logit estimation uses a cumulative lag. Tha it, it reports the probability of lying in round $t$ as a function of having lied or others having lied in any  previous round.  


\noindent\emph{Logit using method B (cumulative lag):}
\begin{align}
\Pr(\text{lied}_{it} = 1) = \Lambda\Big(
  &\ \gamma_1\, \text{any\_group\_lied\_prior}_{it}
   + \gamma_2\, \text{any\_self\_lied\_prior}_{it} \nonumber \\
  &+ \gamma_3\, \mathbf{1}\{T_i = 1\}
   + \gamma_4\, \mathbf{1}\{T_i = 1\} \cdot \text{any\_group\_lied\_prior}_{it} \nonumber \\
  &+ \alpha_s + \alpha_r \Big).
\end{align}
In both estimations, 
$\Lambda(\cdot)$ is the logistic CDF, 
$\alpha_s$ are the super game fixed effects, 
$\alpha_r$ are the round fixed effect,\footnote{The AF Treatment is the reference level, so $\beta_1$ and $\gamma_1$ identify the group mate-lying effect under the AF Treatment, while $\beta_4$ and $\gamma_4$ capture the differential effect under the IF Treatment. Standard errors are clustered at the group level (group $\times$ session $\times$ supe rgame)}
$\text{lied}_{it}$ is an indicator that participant $i$ promised to contribute in round $t$ but contributed below 20 ECUs,
$\text{group\_lied\_lag}_{it}$ is an indicator that any \emph{other} member of $i$'s group lied in round $t{-}1$ (sum-minus-self);
$\text{self\_lied\_lag}_{it}$ is an indicator that participant $i$ lied in round $t{-}1$,
$\text{any\_group\_lied\_prior}_{it}$ is an indicator that any other member of $i$'s group has lied in any earlier round of the current super game, and 
$\text{any\_self\_lied\_prior}_{it}$ is an indicator that participant $i$ has lied in any earlier round of the current supe rgame.

Finally, we analyze the effect of being deceived on individual contribution levels. We use the following specification,
\begin{equation}
    Contribution_{i,t} = \beta_p promise_{i,t} + \beta_s sucker_{i,t} + \beta_t treatment_{i} + \alpha_r + \gamma_s + \varepsilon_{i,t}
\label{eq:contribution_regression}
\end{equation}

where, $promise_{i,t}$ is a binary variable indicating whether the individual made
a promise in that round, and $sucker_{i,t}$ is a binary variable indicating whether the individual was a sucker in any previous round in the current super game.

First, note that there is a significant and positive coefficient on the treatment variable which is different
than the sentiment contribution regression - Table \ref{table:contribution_regression}. This indicates that the treatment effect operates through the first contribution.
Participants contribute 1.62 more ECUs in expectation when they are unable to view the contributions of other group members. The
coefficient on the Sucker variable is significant and negative. This indicates that participants who get ``suckered''
contribute less in future rounds in expectation.

We provide additional analysis on the effect of being suckered using a difference-in-differences style specification.
We use participants who were ``suckered'' as a treated group. The control group are participants who were not ``suckered'',
excluding variation from rounds and supergames via fixed effects. The specification is given by

\begin{equation}
\begin{split}
    Contribution_{i,t} = \sum_{\substack{k = -K \\ k \neq 0}}^{K} \Big[ &\beta_k^{T1} \left( 1\{\tau_{i,t} = k\} \times \text{sucker}_i \times \text{T1}_i \right) \\
    + &\beta_k^{T2} \left( 1\{\tau_{i,t} = k\} \times \text{sucker}_i \times \text{T2}_i \right) \Big] \\
    + &\gamma \, \text{Treatment}_i + \alpha_r + \gamma_s + \varepsilon_{it}
\end{split}
\label{eq:did_contribution}
\end{equation}

Where $\tau_{i,t}$ is the relative round since being suckered, and $\text{T1}_i$ and $\text{T2}_i$ are indicators for
treatment assignment. This specification allows us to recover separate event-time coefficients for each treatment arm. These
coefficients are causal effects given the following assumptions:
\begin{enumerate}
    \item Parallel Trends: Absent the suckering event, suckers and control subjects would have followed the same contribution trajectory.
    While this is fundamentally untestable, pre-trends show no significant differences in trajectory between groups. 
    \item No Anticipation: Subjects don't adjust their contributions prior to being suckered. This is plausible because subjects don't know
    that groupmates will break a promise until after it has happened. Additionally, since participants are never grouped with the same person
    twice, they lack knowledge from prior supergames on other participants' propensity to lie.
\end{enumerate}

\section{Conclusion}
Overall, this study provides a multidimensional analysis of how strategic behavior is fundamentally shaped by the interaction between monitoring structures, verbal communication, and facial emotional cues. By comparing the Individual Feedback (IF) and Aggregate Feedback (AF) treatments, several key findings emerge regarding the resilience and fragility of cooperation. First, we find that while lying is a persistent behavioral trait across both environments, the transparency of the IF treatment serves as a critical moderating force. The logit models demonstrate that a participant’s own prior lying and their groupmates' prior lying are strong predictors of future dishonesty, but this contagion is more pronounced in the AF treatment, where the lack of individual accountability allows deceptive behavior to proliferate under the cover of group anonymity.\\
Second, the "suckering" event leads to a significant and monotonic decrease in future contributions, representing a breakdown in cooperation that is notably difficult to reverse. However, our analysis of the treatment differences shows that this decline is more aggressive in the AF environment. As trust is breached, participants in aggregate-only groups appear to adopt a more cynical, defensive strategy faster than those in IF groups, where the ability to identify a specific defector may offer a slim hope of targeted reconciliation. This suggests that the structural design of the information environment is just as vital for maintaining group trust as the presence of communication itself.\\
In terms of chat and verbal sentiments, the use of VADER sentiment analysis reveals a clear link between verbal tone and economic action, regardless of the treatment group. A positive sentiment maintains a strong, statistically significant correlation ($p < 0.001$) to higher individual contributions, yet the volume of messages does not. This indicates that the quality and emotional alignment of communication, rather than mere frequency, fosters cooperative environments. Interestingly, the IF treatment tends to produce more "credible" sentiment, likely because participants know their individual actions will eventually be revealed, whereas AF participants may use positive sentiment more loosely as a "cheap talk" tool to mask impending free-riding.\\
Finally, the most nuanced findings come from the integration of AFFDEX facial emotion tracking, which highlights a fascinating divergence between internal states and external actions. Participants who lied exhibited a significantly positive facial valence on the results page—the "liar's high"—while showing a significantly negative facial valence during the Pre-Decision Chat phase. This "emotional leakage" suggests that the act of preparing a deception is cognitively and emotionally taxing. Crucially, the visibility of these emotions offers a potential secondary monitoring channel that remains relevant even in AF environments where contribution data is obscured.\\
All in all, while positive verbal sentiment can bolster contributions, its effectiveness is deeply tied to the monitoring treatment in place. By monitoring facial expressions, we can see the true emotional states that chat messages and aggregate data might otherwise hide. The persistent negative impact of being "suckered" underscores that once trust is breached, neither positive sentiment nor structural feedback is easily able to restore the previous equilibrium of high group contribution, especially when the monitoring environment allows for anonymity.

\section{Using Text Embeddings for External Validition}

We use modern text embedding models to convert raw communication to numeric vectors.
This allows us to compare the semantic similarity of messages, especially if we divide messages
into categories, like we have already done. We can then use these embeddings to predict behavior, not
just in our experiment, but also into other scenarios with communication and cooperation.

Let $\mathbb{M}$ be the set of all possible messages. Then the embedding function is

\begin{equation}
    f: \mathbb{M} \rightarrow \mathbb{R}^d
\end{equation}

where $d$ is the embedding dimension which varies by model. We use the function provided by 
OpenAI, text-embedding-3-small, to embed
each participant's messages at the round level. We then group the embeddings into categories
based on the following classifications:

\begin{enumerate}
    \item Cooperative: True if all other group members contribute an average of 20 ECUs.
    \item Promise: True if a promise is detected in the participant's round messages.
    \item Homogeneity: True if all group member contributions differ by no more than 1 ECU.
    \item Round-liar: True if the participant lied in the current round.
    \item Cumulative-liar: True if the participant lied in the current supergame.
\end{enumerate}

For each of these classifications, we compute the centroid difference between the average true and false case.
This is simply the distance between the average vectors in each category. We then normalize this vector to remove
influence from the length of message. These normalized centroid vectors can be interpretted as the semantic difference
between cooperative and non-cooperative states, or the semantic difference between homogenous vs heterogenous contribution states etc. 

Now that we have centroids representing semantic differences between states, we can project any message embedding onto the centroid
vector, using the dot product, to get a scalar value. This scalar projection value can be intrepretted as the degree of semantic
similarity between the message and the centroid. In other words, ``How cooperative is this message?'' or ``How similar is this message to
messages sent in a homogenous contribution environment?''

We implement this method by projecting each participant's messages in each round onto each of our centroid vectors. We then specify separate regressions,
regressing group contribution totals on the separate projection scores. Table \ref{table:group_contribution_embedding_regression}
displays the results from these regressions. The coefficient on cooperative and homogeneity are positive and significant. This indicates
that individuals who communicate similarly to cooperative groups or homogenous groups, tend to have higher group contributions. The coefficients
on round-liar and cumulative-liar are negative and significant indicating that individuals who communicate similarly to liars, tend to have lower 
group contributions. Note that these coefficients are greater than 25, meaning that the liar effect spreads beyond just the lying individual. This 
is consistent with what we found in Figure \ref{fig:did_contribution}.

These coefficients are somewhat circular. The cooperative projection score coefficient is positive partially because we defined a cooperative state
as one where the contribution meets a minimum threshold. These coefficients demonstrate internal validity. The projection scores are significant, meaning
participants communicate semantically different depending on the state of the group. We then can take our centroid vectors from \textit{this} experiment,
and apply them to other experimental, or real world, data.
