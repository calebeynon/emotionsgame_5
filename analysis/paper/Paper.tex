\section*{Abstract}
This paper studies how information structure and emotions affect cooperation in infinitely repeated public goods games with communication. We conduct a laboratory experiment in which groups of four participants play a series of probabilistically-ending supergames. The key treatment variations are in feedback: in one treatment participants observe both individual contributions and the group total, while in the other they observe only the group total. In addition to contribution decisions and chat messages, we record facial expressions to measure emotional responses using facial emotion recognition and text sentiment analysis. Preliminary results show higher average contributions when only aggregate contributions are observable. Communication helps identify behavioral patterns such as promise-making, lying, and “sucker” behavior. While promises themselves do not significantly predict contributions due to ceiling effects, being exploited by a lying group member significantly reduces subsequent contributions. Sentiment analysis further shows that more positive communication is associated with higher contributions, highlighting the role of emotions and communication in sustaining cooperation under different monitoring structures.

\section{Introduction}
Cooperation in public-goods environments critically depends on both communication as well as the information individuals have about one another’s behavior. A large body of experimental literature shows that allowing subjects to communicate can substantially increase contributions relative to settings without communication, even when communication is cheap talk and non-binding. At the same time, the degree of transparency surrounding individual actions—specifically, whether individual contributions are observable or only aggregate outcomes are revealed—has been shown to shape incentives, social norms, and the sustainability of cooperation. Despite extensive study of these two mechanisms independently, less is known about how communication interacts with informational visibility to influence cooperative behavior over time.

In many real-world collective action problems, individuals can communicate freely while facing varying degrees of transparency about others’ behavior. In some settings, such as small teams or committees, individual contributions are highly visible. In others, such as large organizations or anonymous online platforms, only aggregate outcomes are observed, obscuring individual responsibility. Understanding how cooperation emerges and evolves under these different informational regimes—particularly when communication is present—is essential for explaining variation in cooperative outcomes across institutional environments.

In this paper, we present a experimental study to explain how the visibility of individual contributions affects cooperation, communication, and contribution dynamics in a repeated public-goods game with endogenous communication. We employ a between-subjects design with two treatments that vary in terms of informational transparency. In the \textbf{Feedback} treatment, subjects observe the individual contributions of all group members at the end of each period. In the \textbf{No Feedback}treatment, subjects observe only the aggregate group contribution. In both treatments, subjects communicate freely via an on-screen chat before each decision. This design allows us to isolate how transparency at the individual versus group level interacts with communication to shape cooperative behavior.

Our experimental environment incorporates several features that reflect realistic collective decision-making settings. Subjects interact repeatedly within segments but are rematched across segments, limiting the scope for long-term reputation building while preserving short-run strategic considerations. Communication is unrestricted and occurs prior to each contribution decision, allowing groups to coordinate, make promises, and express approval or disapproval. Finally, the random-ending structure within each segment mitigates endgame effects and encourages forward-looking behavior throughout the interaction.

The contribution of this paper is threefold. First, we provide causal evidence on how informational visibility alters contribution levels and dynamics when communication is available. While prior studies have examined the effects of communication or visibility in isolation, our design directly compares individual-level and aggregate-level transparency within an otherwise identical communicative environment. Second, we analyze how communication content and patterns differ across informational regimes, shedding light on the mechanisms through which transparency influences cooperation. Third, we examine how cooperation evolves over time within and across segments, highlighting differences in stability, responsiveness to deviations, and decay in contributions.

The emotional state of a player can be a significant driver of how they make certain decisions. With the public good framework, with communication allowed, we are interested in learning two things: Does communication, and subsequent match or mismatch of action trigger emotional response in participants. If emotions are present, do they get reflected in the behavior? We record our participants through the duration of our study and analyze these recorded videos via a facial recognition technology to see if there is any coorelation between verbal communication and facial expressions, and between emotions and contributions in the public goods game.

\section{Related Literature}

Communication significantly enhances cooperation in public-goods and social dilemma settings. Early experimental studies demonstrate that allowing participants to communicate—even when communication is non-binding and has no direct payoff consequences—leads to higher contributions and improved group outcomes relative to no-communication baselines \cite{Dawes1977_Communication}. These findings challenged the prediction of standard self-interest models and highlighted the importance of social interaction and norm formation in collective action problems.

Subsequent work has confirmed the generality of this effect across a wide range of institutional settings. \cite{Sally1995_Meta} provides a comprehensive meta-analysis of experiments conducted over several decades, showing that communication consistently increases cooperation and reduces free-riding behavior. Communication allows participants to coordinate expectations, articulate shared goals, and invoke social norms, thereby mitigating strategic uncertainty and fostering trust.

More recent research has shifted focus toward understanding the mechanisms through which communication operates. \cite{Brandts2019_Communication} survey the experimental literature on communication and emphasize that its effectiveness depends critically on institutional details, including the information available to participants and the structure of interaction. Communication can facilitate cooperation by enabling coordination on strategies, reinforcing social norms, and expressing approval or disapproval, but its impact is not uniform across environments.

The timing and structure of communication also matter. \cite{Oprea2014_ContinuousTime} study continuous-time communication in a public-goods experiment and find that richer communication opportunities improve coordination and reduce early misalignment of beliefs. Their results suggest that communication serves as an active coordination device rather than merely a symbolic signal of intent.

When individual contributions are observable, participants can condition their behavior on others’ actions, enabling informal reciprocity, reputation building, and norm enforcement. \cite{Fischbacher2001_Conditional} provide seminal evidence that many individuals are conditionally cooperative, increasing their contributions when others contribute more and reducing them when others free ride. Such behavior relies fundamentally on the ability to observe or infer others’ actions.

Visibility also interacts with social preferences and framing effects. \cite{Andreoni1995_Cooperation} shows that cooperation can be influenced by how decisions are framed, highlighting the role of moral considerations and social image. When individual behavior is observable, social image concerns and accountability are likely to be more salient, strengthening incentives to cooperate.

More broadly, the ability to monitor and respond to individual behavior has been shown to support the emergence of informal governance structures. \cite{Ostrom1992_Covenants} demonstrate that groups can sustain cooperation through self-governance mechanisms even in the absence of formal enforcement, provided that individual actions are sufficiently transparent. Similarly, \cite{Gachter2007_Norms} show that mechanisms enabling norm enforcement generate long-run benefits for cooperation, underscoring the importance of accountability in sustaining collective action.

In contrast, when only aggregate outcomes are observable, individual responsibility is blurred. This may weaken conditional cooperation and reduce the effectiveness of informal sanctions, leading to more rapid decay in contributions.

While both communication and informational visibility have been shown to promote cooperation, fewer studies examine how these mechanisms interact. Communication may substitute for visibility by allowing individuals to signal intentions and coordinate behavior, or it may depend on visibility to be effective by enabling verification and accountability.

\cite{Haruvy2017_CommunicationVisibility} directly investigate this interaction by varying the visibility of individual contributions in a public-goods game with communication. They find that communication is substantially more effective when individual contributions are observable, suggesting that transparency and communication are complementary. When individual actions are hidden, communication alone is less successful in sustaining cooperation.

\cite{Brandts2019_Communication} similarly emphasize that the impact of communication depends on the surrounding informational environment, noting that communication without the ability to attribute behavior may be insufficient to support stable cooperation. However, existing studies provide limited insight into how communication content and contribution dynamics differ across informational regimes, particularly in settings with repeated interaction and rematching.

Finally, this paper relates to the literature on dynamic incentives and repeated interaction in public-goods experiments. Random-ending designs are commonly used to mitigate endgame effects and encourage forward-looking behavior. \cite{Isaac1988_Communication}study the dynamics of public-goods provision using random continuation probabilities and show that such structures influence contribution trajectories and strategic adjustment over time.

By combining repeated interaction within segments, random rematching across segments, and endogenous communication, our design allows us to study how cooperation evolves dynamically under different informational regimes while limiting long-term reputation effects.

While prior work establishes that both communication and visibility matter for cooperation, we show that the effectiveness of communication critically depends on the ability to attribute behavior to individuals. Moreover, we document how informational transparency shapes not only contribution levels but also the nature and function of communication itself.

\section{Experimental Design}
Our study employs a between-subjects design with two treatments that vary the degree of informational visibility in a standard public-goods environment. Across the two treatments, we manipulate whether subjects can observe both the individual contributions of their group members or only the aggregate contribution of the group, i.e. in the "Feedback" treatment, each subject observes the contributions of all of their group members at the end of every round \footnote{Refer to Appendix: Results. Each participant can see their contribution and also exactly how much everyone else in that group contrubuted}, while in the "No Feedback" treatment, subjects see only the total contribution placed into the group account, without any breakdown of who contributed how much. This manipulation allows us to study how transparency—either at the individual or group level—affects cooperative behavior, communication patterns, and contribution dynamics.

Participants were paired into groups of 4 people. They were randomly assigned to one of the two treatments and would be in the same treatment for the entire session. Each participant was assigned a unique letter at the beginning of the session. In the "Feedback" treatement, these letters were used to allow participants to observe individual behavior across their group. 
The experiment consisted of five segments, each made up of varying number of rounds. Groups were re-matched across segments in a way that no subject remained with the same set of partners across segments. Each segment followed a random-ending structure based on the continuation probabilities in \cite{lugovskyy2010experimental} (Sequence 1). The sequence of period lengths was (3, 4, 3, 7, 5), totaling 22 rounds for the duration of a session. Each participant played all 22 rounds, resulting in 110 total rounds across the five segments. 

At the beginning of every round, each participant received 25 ECU (experimental currency units) in their private account. Participants decided how many of these points to allocate to the group account. After each round, subjects viewed the earnings from that round alongside the group account total (and individual contributions- in the Feedback treatment). Our unique addition included allowing participants to communicate as they moved through this framework of the public goods game. Each participant would chat with the other members of their group before they decided their contribution to the group vs private account. Chat page restarted for each round, and the total time allocated for chat decreased over the course of a segment \footnote{Initial chat time was set to one minute.}. Subjects could freely exchange messages but were prohibited from sharing identifying information or using profanity. All communication occurred through an on-screen chat box \footnote{Refer to Appendix: Chat Page to see a sample of how the chat box appeared on screen for participants}.

After completing all five segments, one segment was randomly selected to determine each participant’s final payoff. We conducted a total of 10 sessions, each with 16 participants, yielding 160 subjects overall. The average payoff was xxx , converted at the rate of 1 ECU = 0.1 dollars?

\subsection{Regression Analysis}

We start by analyzing the dynamic effect of individual contributions across treatments. We estimate the following model subsetted
by treatment using two-step difference GMM[CITE Arrellano and Bond].  
\begin{equation}
\begin{split}
    \Delta Contribution_{i,t}
    &= \beta_1 \Delta Contribution_{i,t-1} + \beta_2 \Delta Contribution_{i,t-2} \\
    &\quad + \beta_{pos} \Delta D^+_{i,t-1} + \beta_{neg} \Delta D^-_{i,t-1}
    + \sum_{r=1}^{5} \beta_{Rr} \cdot 1\{Round_{t} = r\} \\
    &\quad + \psi \Delta Segment_{t} + \varepsilon_{i,t}
\end{split}
\label{eq:dynamic_reg}
\end{equation}
Where $\Delta Contribution_{i,t} = Contribution_{i,t} - Contribution_{i,t-1}$ and $Contribution_{i,t}$ is the 
round level individual contribution to the group account. $D^+_{i,t}$ and $D^-_{i,t}$ are binary variables indicating
whether the individual contribution was greater or less than the group mean for round $t$. $Round_t$ and $Segment_t$
are variables for position in time within the experiment. We use $\Delta Contribution_{i,t-2}$, $\Delta Contribution_{i,t-3}$, and $\Delta Contribution_{i,t-4}$ 
as the lagged instruments.

\subsection{Classifying Behavior}

During the experiment, we allowed participants to communicate freely with little restriction. This allowed for text analysis across several dimensions.
We first start by presenting a classification of text and corresponding behavior that relates to our hypotheses. Historically, classifying text has been arduous. However, we utilize recent advancements in large language models
to perform this task. Specifically, we are interested in identifying whether a participant makes a promise to other group members.
Using \cite{openai_gpt5mini_2025}'s GPT-5-mini model, we classify a participant's message as a promise or not a promise \footnote{ We use the same prompt for all participants. This prompt includes the following elements: a brief explanation of the public goods game, a list of examples of promises, a message to classify, and the 
preceding messages in the conversation. The model is explicitly told to take the participant's message in the context of the whole conversation.}

By classifying the messages from participants, we can classify their behavior as well. We create two separate classifications at the participant-round level.
First, we classify a participant as a ``liar'' if they made a promise, but later failed to contribute more than 20 ECUs in the next contribution decision. Each participant begins each segment without the ``liar'' label, but the label persists for the rest of the segment once they first become a liar. We then classify a participant as a ``sucker'' if they contributed the maximum (25 points) in a round where a groupmate lied. Similar to the liar label, the sucker label persists for the rest of the segment once they first become a sucker.

\begin{equation}

\subsection{Communication Sentiment}

We analyze the effect of communication sentiment on individual contributions. We use the following specification,
\begin{equation}
    Contribution_{i,t} = \beta_s sentiment_{i,t} + \beta_t treatment_{i} + \beta_n n\_messages_{i,t}+ \alpha_r + \gamma_s + \varepsilon_{i,t}
\label{eq:contribution_sentiment}
\end{equation}

where $Contribution_{i,t}$ is the round level individual contribution, $treatment_{i}$ is a binary variable for treatment 2,
$sentiment_{i,t}$ is the mean sentiment for participant $i$ in round, segment $t$, $n\_messages_{i,t}$ is the number of messages sent
by participant $i$ in $t$, $\alpha_r$ are round fixed effects and $\gamma_s$ are segment fixed effects. Table \ref{table:contribution_regression}
presents the results from this regression. Sentiment has a positive and significant coefficient of 2.182, indicating that a positive valence
is associated with higher contributions in expectation. The number of messages sent by an individual is not significant. A limitation of our experimental design
is that the first communication opportunity does not happen until \textit{after} the first contribution, thus those contributions are excluded
from this regression. 

\subsection{Classification Effect}

We analyze the effect of being deceived on individual contribution levels. We use the following specification,
\begin{equation}
    Contribution_{i,t} = \beta_p promise_{i,t} + \beta_s sucker_{i,t} + \beta_t treatment_{i} + \alpha_r + \gamma_s + \varepsilon_{i,t}
\label{eq:contribution_regression}
\end{equation}
where $promise_{i,t}$ is a binary variable indicating whether the individual made
a promise in that round, and $sucker_{i,t}$ is a binary variable indicating whether the individual was a sucker in any previous round in the current segment.

First, note that there is a significant and positive coefficient on the Treatment variable which is different
than the sentiment contribution regression. This indicates that the treatment effect operates through the first contribution.
Participants contribute 1.62 more ECUs in expectation when they are unable to view the contributions of other group members. The
coefficient on the Sucker variable is significant and negative. This indicates that participants who get ``suckered''
contribute less in future rounds in expectation.

We provide additional analysis on the effect of being suckered using a difference-in-differences style specification.
We use participants who were ``suckered'' as a treated group. The control group are participants who were not ``suckered'',
excluding variation from rounds and segments via fixed effects. The specification is given by

\begin{equation}
    Contribution_{i,t} = \sum_{\substack{k = -K \\ k \neq 0}}^{K} \beta_k \left( 1\{\tau_{i,t} = k\} \times \text{sucker}_i \right) + \gamma \, \text{Treatment}_i + \alpha_r + \gamma_s +    
    \varepsilon_{it} 
    \label{eq:did_contribution}
\end{equation}

Where $\tau_{i,t}$ is the relative round since being suckered. This specification allows us to recover a coefficient
for each round since being suckered. These coefficients are causal effects given the following assumptions:
\begin{enumerate}
    \item Parallel Trends: Absent the suckering event, suckers and control subjects would have followed the same contribution trajectory.
    While this is fundamentally untestable, pre-trends show no significant differences in trajectory between groups. 
    \item No Anticipation: Subjects don't adjust their contributions prior to being suckered. This is plausible because subjects don't know
    that groupmates will break a promise until after it has happened. Additionally, since participants are never grouped with the same person
    twice, they lack knowledge from prior segments on other participants' propensity to lie.
\end{enumerate}

Coefficients following the event are significantly negative in every round and monotonically decrease. This suggests there is some

\subsection{Facial Emotions}

In addition to observing communication between participants, we observe facial emotions during the experiment
through a webcam. We then use the Affective AFFDEX software to convert raw video into time stamped facial emotion observations.
We observe continuous emotion variables, such as valence, anger, or frustration, and indicator variables for eye rolls, lip pursing, etc. 

One could typically expect facial emotions and communication sentiment to be positively correlated in cases where someone is telling the truth.
In the case where a participant is lying, it seems plausible that sentiment and emotion could differ as they attempt to deceive their groupmates.
To create the comparison between emotions and sentiment, we first normalize each observation using the min-max normalization method across the entire sample.
We only analyze sentiment and emotions during the communication \textit{prior} to the contribution, and the contribution itself. Differences are not taken
across rounds. 

Figure \ref{fig:emotion_sentiment_gap_by_liar_status} displays the normalized valence emotion and sentiment split by liar status.
In the case where a participant is not lying, there is no significant difference between the facial emotions and communication sentiment. 
However, when a participant is lying, their communication sentiment is significantly lower than facial emotions. One possible explanation is that
liars try to communicate similarly to everyone else (typically very neutral), but know that they will defect and receive a higher payoff. This 
would be observable in their facial expression, but not in their public communication.
\begin{figure}[htbp]
    \centering
    \includegraphics[width=\linewidth]{emotion_sentiment_gap_by_liar_status.png}
    \caption{Comparison of normalized emotions and sentiment by liar status.}
    \label{fig:emotion_sentiment_gap_by_liar_status}

Additionally, we test whether the gap in emotions and sentiment is predictive of a participant lying. We use
the following logit specification,

\begin{equation}
    \Pr(\text{Lied}_{it} = 1 \mid \text{Promise}_{it} = 1) = \Lambda\left(\beta_1 \, \text{Gap}_{it} + \alpha_r + \gamma_s\right)
    \label{eq:logit_liar}
\end{equation}

Where $\Lambda(\cdot)$ is the logistic function $\frac{e^x}{1+e^x}$, and $\text{Gap}_{it}$ is the min-max normalized emotion-sentiment gap (facial valence minus chat sentiment). The
liar model conditions on the subsample of participant-rounds where a promise was made. After estimation and inference, we find that
$\hat{\beta_1} = 1.034$ is not significant, failing to demonstrate the ability of the emotion sentiment gap to predict lying, likely
due to lack of power.

\subsection{Using Text Embeddings for External Validition}

We use modern text embedding models to convert raw communication to numeric vectors.
This allows us to compare the semantic similarity of messages, especially if we divide messages
into categories, like we have already done. We can then use these embeddings to predict behavior, not
just in our experiment, but also into other scenarios with communication and cooperation.

Let $\mathbb{M}$ be the set of all possible messages. Then the embedding function is

\begin{equation}
    f: \mathbb{M} \rightarrow \mathbb{R}^d
\end{equation}

where $d$ is the embedding dimension which varies by model. We use the function provided by 
OpenAI, text-embedding-3-small, to embed
each participant's messages at the round level. We then group the embeddings into categories
based on the following classifications:

\begin{enumerate}
    \item Cooperative: True if all other group members contribute an average of 20 ECUs.
    \item Promise: True if a promise is detected in the participant's round messages.
    \item Homogeneity: True if all group member contributions differ by no more than 1 ECU.
    \item Round-liar: True if the participant lied in the current round.
    \item Cumulative-liar: True if the participant lied in the current segment.
\end{enumerate}

For each of these classifications, we compute the centroid difference between the average true and false case.
This is simply the distance between the average vectors in each category. We then normalize this vector to remove
influence from the length of message. These normalized centroid vectors can be interpretted as the semantic difference
between cooperative and non-cooperative states, or the semantic difference between homogenous vs heterogenous contribution states etc. 

Now that we have centroids representing semantic differences between states, we can project any message embedding onto the centroid
vector, using the dot product, to get a scalar value. This scalar projection value can be intrepretted as the degree of semantic
similarity between the message and the centroid. In other words, ``How cooperative is this message?'' or ``How similar is this message to
messages sent in a homogenous contribution environment?''

We implement this method by projecting each participant's messages in each round onto each of our centroid vectors. We then specify separate regressions,
regressing group contribution totals on the separate projection scores. Table \ref{table:group_contribution_embedding_regression}
displays the results from these regressions. The coefficient on cooperative and homogeneity are positive and significant. This indicates
that individuals who communicate similarly to cooperative groups or homogenous groups, tend to have higher group contributions. The coefficients
on round-liar and cumulative-liar are negative and significant indicating that individuals who communicate similarly to liars, tend to have lower 
group contributions. Note that these coefficients are greater than 25, meaning that the liar effect spreads beyond just the lying individual. This 
is consistent with what we found in Figure \ref{fig:did_contribution}.

These coefficients are somewhat circular. The cooperative projection score coefficient is positive partially because we defined a cooperative state
as one where the contribution meets a minimum threshold. These coefficients demonstrate internal validity. The projection scores are significant, meaning
participants communicate semantically different depending on the state of the group. We then can take our centroid vectors from \textit{this} experiment,
and apply them to other experimental, or real world, data.

\subsection{Instructions for the Experiment}
\begin{center}
\textbf{Welcome}\\
\end{center}
Welcome to our study. At this time, please make sure you have turned off and put away all personal electronic devices. This study will take no more than 75 minutes. You will be paid your earnings from the study in addition to the \$7.50 participation payment in cash. Your earnings will depend on the decisions you and others make in this study, so it is important that you fully understand these instructions. These earnings will be denominated in Experimental Currency Units (or ECUs for short). At the end of the study, ECUs will be converted into dollars at the exchange rate of 10 ECUs for \$1.00 and will be paid to you privately in cash. If you have a question at any point, please raise your hand.\\


\textbf{Structure}\\
You will interact with other participants in this session through a computer program. Your personal identifying information will not be revealed to anyone. To the participants that you will interact with, you will be referred to as a unique alphabet letter randomly assigned to you. For this session, you will be referred to as

\begin{center}
    \textbf{“Participant None”}
\end{center}

This study consists of 5 \textbf{segments}. At the beginning of each segment, all participants will be randomly placed in a group of 4. Each group will remain the same throughout the segment. When a new segment begins, all participants will be randomly placed in a new group of 4. In these 5 segments, you will be placed in 5 different groups and will not interact with the same persons in different segments.\\
\textbf{Ending of Segments}
Each segment consists of an uncertain number of rounds. The number of rounds in a segment is randomly selected by the computer in the following manner: after each round, there is an 80\% chance that another round of the same segment will follow and there is a 20\% chance that the segment will end after that round. You can think about this in the following way:



In each round, the computer will randomly select a number from 1 to 5, if the selected number is from 1 to 4, the next round will follow. If the selected number is 5, the segment will end.\\

\textbf{Tokens and allocation}
\begin{itemize}
    \item Each participant is endowed with 25 tokens at the beginning of each round and has to allocate these tokens between a \textbf{Private Account} and a \textbf{Group Account}. Each participant has their own Private Account and there is only one Group Account for each group.
\item You will earn 1.0 ECU for each token you allocate to your Private Account. Each token allocated to the Group Account yields a return of 1.6 ECUs. Everyone in the group will receive an equal share of earnings from the Group Account. Since there are 4 participants in a group, each participant will receive

\begin{center}
    \textbf{$\frac{1}{4}$ x 1.6 x the total number of tokens in the the Group Account}
\end{center}

in ECUs.

\item We would like to emphasize each participant in a group will receive the same amount of ECUs from the Group Account, even though each allocated a different number of tokens to the Group Account
\end{itemize}
\textbf{Decisions}\\
Each round consists of 2 stages.

\begin{itemize}
    \item In stage 1, you will divide your 25 tokens between the Private Account and the Group Account. Below, you will enter the number of tokens you want to allocate to the Group Account. (Enter a whole number between 0 and 25 to allocate to the Group Account). The computer will automatically show how many tokens are allocated to the Private Account.
\item In stage 2, you will view the results of the round and chat with your group members.
\item You will be able to see \textbf{the total number of tokens in the group account} and \textbf{each group member's allocation to the group account}.
\item You will be able to chat with your group members for up to 90 seconds. Please avoid profanities and offensive language. This will not be tolerated. Do not use any language other than English. Also, avoid revealing your current seating location or personal identifying information about yourself such as your name, age, major, gender, etc.
\item Even though you will make decisions in many rounds and the computer will calculate your earnings in ECUs for all of these rounds, the computer will randomly select only 1 segment to calculate your cash payment. The computer will use the sum of your earnings from all rounds in the selected segment and convert it into dollars at the exchange rate of 10 ECUs for \$1.00. You will know which segment is selected for your cash earnings at the end of the study.
\end{itemize}
